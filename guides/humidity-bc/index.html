Humidity Bias Correction Guide#

Background: two ways to express atmospheric humidity#

The atmosphere can hold a limited amount of water vapour, and that limit rises strongly with temperature. Near-surface humidity is routinely reported in two different forms:

Dew-point temperature (d2m, unit K or °C) is the temperature to which a parcel of air must be cooled at constant pressure before water vapour begins to condense. At the dew point the air is exactly saturated — its actual vapour pressure equals the saturation vapour pressure. ERA5 stores this as the 2 m dew-point temperature.

Specific humidity (huss, unit kg kg⁻¹) is the mass of water vapour per unit mass of moist air. It is the quantity most directly useful for computing latent heat fluxes and for comparing model thermodynamics. CMIP6 models output this variable directly.

DatasetVariableUnitsDescription
ERA5d2mK2 m dew-point temperature
ERA5spPaSurface pressure (companion variable)
CMIP6husskg kg⁻¹Near-surface specific humidity

Because bias correction requires a common currency, ERA5 d2m must be converted to specific humidity before the correction is applied.

Step 1 — Convert ERA5 d2m to specific humidity#

1a. Saturation vapour pressure from dew-point temperature (Buck equation)#

At the dew-point temperature the air is exactly saturated, so the actual vapour pressure equals the saturation vapour pressure at that temperature. The Buck (1981) equation gives the saturation vapour pressure over a liquid water surface as a function of temperature:

e(Td) = 6.1121 × exp( 17.368 × Td_C / (238.88 + Td_C) )   [hPa]

where Td_C = d2m [K] − 273.15 is the dew-point in °C.

Constants explained:

ConstantValueMeaning
6.1121hPaSaturation vapour pressure of water at 0 °C — the reference point of the Clausius–Clapeyron curve. The formula returns this value when Td_C = 0.
17.368dimensionlessEmpirical scaling of the Clausius–Clapeyron exponent, fitted to laboratory measurements of saturated water vapour over a liquid surface in the 0–50 °C range.
238.88°CEmpirical offset in the denominator; together with 17.368 it shapes how steeply vapour pressure rises with temperature. Physically it absorbs the effective activation energy of evaporation from the Clausius–Clapeyron equation.

The exponential form is a simplified solution to the Clausius–Clapeyron differential equation, which states that the slope of the saturation vapour pressure curve is proportional to e / T² (latent heat and gas-law terms absorbed into the empirical constants). Buck’s coefficients are fitted to measured data and are more accurate than earlier Magnus-formula variants, particularly in the 0–50 °C range relevant for NE-Atlantic forcing.

1b. Specific humidity from vapour pressure#

Once the actual vapour pressure e is known, specific humidity follows from the ideal gas law. Water vapour (molar mass M_w = 18.015 g mol⁻¹) and dry air (molar mass M_d = 28.964 g mol⁻¹) obey Dalton’s law of partial pressures:

q = ε × e / (p_hPa − (1 − ε) × e)

Constants explained:

SymbolValueMeaning
ε0.622Ratio of molar masses M_w / M_d = 18.015 / 28.964. Not an empirical fit — it is an exact physical constant. Water vapour is lighter than dry air, so humid air is less dense than dry air at the same pressure and temperature.
p_hPaPa / 100Total surface pressure in hPa (ERA5 sp divided by 100).
ehPaActual vapour pressure from step 1a.

The denominator p_hPa − (1 − ε) × e is the effective partial pressure of the dry-air component. At typical atmospheric conditions (e ≈ 10–30 hPa, p ≈ 1000 hPa) the correction term (1 − ε) × e is small (< 1 %), so the simpler approximation q ≈ ε × e / p introduces negligible error. The full form is used here for correctness.

The result q is in kg kg⁻¹ — directly comparable to CMIP6 huss.

Note on surface pressure: ERA5 sp is required as a companion variable. Set sp_variable: sp and sp_path pointing to the ERA5 surface-pressure file. Using the standard atmosphere value p₀ = 1013.25 hPa in place of actual sp introduces < 1 % error in q for mid-latitude conditions — acceptable if sp is unavailable, but the full pressure field is preferred for coastal and orographically complex regions.

Step 2 — Bias correction (QDM, multiplicative)#

Specific humidity is a non-negative, bounded ratio. It therefore uses multiplicative Quantile Delta Mapping (QDM) — the same treatment applied to precipitation (pr).

Input:
  ref    = q derived from ERA5 d2m + sp  (calibration period, e.g. 1990–2019)
  hist   = CMIP6 historical huss         (calibration period)
  future = CMIP6 future huss             (projection period)

Output:
  huss_corrected  (projection period, kg kg⁻¹)

Multiplicative QDM works in two steps per future time step:

  1. Compute the ratio future / hist_quantile_match — the model-projected relative change in humidity, which is preserved.
  2. Apply that ratio to the corresponding ERA5 reference quantile — the corrected value inherits the ERA5 climatological distribution while retaining the CMIP6 trend signal.

This is preferable to additive correction for humidity because relative changes (e.g. +5 % above climatology) are physically more meaningful than additive offsets, and the multiplicative form cannot produce negative values.

Step 3 — Optional: back-convert to dew-point temperature#

If the ocean model requires d2m rather than huss, invert the conversion:

e = q × p_hPa / (ε + (1 − ε) × q)        # vapour pressure [hPa]

Td_C = 238.88 × ln(e / 6.1121) / (17.368 − ln(e / 6.1121))

d2m [K] = Td_C + 273.15

This is the algebraic inverse of steps 1a and 1b.

Configuration#

Add huss to config/bc.yaml (see also config/bias_correction_example.yaml):

variables:
  - name: huss
    kind: "*"             # multiplicative — specific humidity is non-negative
    regridding:
      method: bilinear    # intensive quantity (ratio), not a flux

reference:
  huss:
    source: local
    path:        "/data/ERA5/era5_d2m_{year}.nc"
    variable:    d2m          # triggers d2m → q conversion automatically
    sp_variable: sp
    sp_path:     "/data/ERA5/era5_sp_{year}.nc"

historical:
  huss:
    source: cmip6
    uri: "cmip6://MPI-ESM1-2-HR/historical/huss/day/r1i1p1f1"

future_model:
  huss:
    source: cmip6
    uri: "cmip6://MPI-ESM1-2-HR/{scenario}/huss/day/r1i1p1f1"

The sp_variable key is the trigger: when present, bias_correct_forcings.py calls d2m_to_huss(d2m, sp) before passing the result to the QDM pipeline. No extra flags are needed.

Regridding note: bilinear is correct for huss. Conservative interpolation — which preserves the area-integrated total — is reserved for extensive flux variables (pr, rsds, rlds). Specific humidity is an intensive state variable like tas, uas, vas, and psl, so bilinear interpolation of the point value is appropriate.

Temporal disaggregation#

If sub-daily forcing is required, huss should be disaggregated for consistency with the other continuously-varying variables (tas, wind, psl). The diurnal amplitude of specific humidity is modest (~5–15 % of the daily mean) but it feeds directly into the latent heat flux, so omitting it while disaggregating tas would introduce an inconsistency.

Add a huss block to the disaggregation.variables section:

disaggregation:
  variables:
    huss:
      era5_path:     "/data/ERA5/era5_d2m_{year}.nc"
      era5_variable: d2m          # triggers d2m → q conversion before diurnal ratio
      sp_variable:   sp
      sp_path:       "/data/ERA5/era5_sp_{year}.nc"

The sp_variable key triggers the same d2m_to_huss() conversion used in the reference-loading stage. The diurnal ratio is then built from sub-daily q = f(d2m, sp) and applied multiplicatively to the daily bias-corrected huss:

huss_hourly(t, h) = huss_daily_bc(t) × ERA5_q_diurnal_ratio(DOY(t), h)

where ERA5_q_diurnal_ratio = ERA5_q_hour / ERA5_q_daily_mean.

No scale_factor is needed: the ratio computation cancels any unit offset between the raw d2m input and the derived q.

Running#

# Include huss in a full bias correction run (correction + disaggregation)
bc correct --config config/bc.yaml

# Correct only huss (skip disaggregation)
bc correct --config config/bc.yaml --variable huss --steps correct

# Disaggregate only (uses previously written BC files)
bc correct --config config/bc.yaml --variable huss --steps disaggregate

# Run diagnostics for huss
bc diagnostics --config config/bc.yaml --variable huss

Key references#

  • Buck (1981), New equations for computing vapor pressure and enhancement factor, J. Appl. Meteor. 20, 1527–1532. — Source of the 6.1121 / 17.368 / 238.88 constants; optimised for the 0–50 °C range over liquid water.
  • Alduchov & Eskridge (1996), Improved Magnus Form Approximation of Saturation Vapor Pressure, J. Appl. Meteor. 35, 601–609. — Alternative coefficients for the same functional form.
  • Cannon et al. (2015), Bias Correction of GCM Precipitation by Quantile Mapping, J. Climate 28, 6938–6959. — Derivation and properties of the QDM algorithm.
